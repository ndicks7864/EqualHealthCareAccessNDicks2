Comparing The Two Articles:

Both of these articles, while fighting for the same issue, take a totally different approach on how to address the reader of the matter at hand. Within article one, the author sticks to a more sophisticated tone as they use factual evidence to back their reasoning and explanations behind teacher salary struggles. Whereas, in article two the author writes with passion and desire as they explain to the reader struggles with teacher salaries; instead of providing evidence to back their reasoning they use strong, harsh diction to criticize the system and pay which teachers must work with. Both articles make strong arguments about teacher salaries but article two can be considered skewed due to it favoring one side of the argument and lack evidence while article one can be looked upon as stronger due to the overwhelming use of evidence and openness to both sides of the argument.



Which is the better article?

Out of the two articles given the first article seems to be the most informative article. Article one, while still having bias, backed their ideas on factual points/evidence unlike the second article which seemed to base the majority of its writing on personal opinion. I do feel it is more trustworthy because of the significant amount of evidence that is provided along with provided quotations from educated people. For example, in article one the author states, “Nearly 60% of people in a Ipsos/USA Today survey released Sept. 12 think teachers are underpaid, while a majority of both Republicans and Democrats believe they have the right to strike” (paragraph eleven). Another example where the author of article one provides adequate evidence is when they state, “Nationwide, the estimated average public-school teacher’s salary is now $58,950, according to the National Center for Education Statistics…” (paragraph sixteen). Both sentences within the article show the author's use of evidence to support their backing on teacher salaries; it is this type of evidence that allows me to put trust in this article. This however does not make the other article, article two, incorrect it just makes it a less credible source, but not wrong.



Taking Precautions

While researching and reading about the issue of equal health care access, I need to be aware/cautious of underlying bias in the articles that I am receiving information from. I also need to be aware/cautious that the information I am receiving may not be from a credible source and therefore might be incorrect or skewed. Another caution I need to take while learning about my issue is that I do not incorporate my own bias into the information I am putting out. By keeping myself from including my own bias, informing myself of the possibilities that articles can include underlying bias, and remembering that articles can give out incorrect/skewed information I can avoid transferring and including that bias within my own work and therefore put forward work that focuses on the issue at hand along with the statistics/facts that surround it. My work can then be considered a credible source - with as minimum bias as possible.
